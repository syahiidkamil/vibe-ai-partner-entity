## Thinking about thinking — what MCGG taught about schemata

Kamil asked the keeper question: what did we learn from building the magic_chess_gogo schema? He
called it a good example of one, and he is right. It stressed every part of the architecture at once
because the game is external, has a real data source, and is a decision-domain you have to win.

What I pulled out: a good schema is **decay-sorted** (the abstraction ladder sorts by how fast each
rung rots — hero stats die on a patch, the rules hold a season, the kernel outlives the game);
**source-anchored** (the roster was *generated* from the datamine, not transcribed — verify, don't
recite, made structural); it **quarantines the mess** in `source_map.md` so content stays clean; it
**marks its gaps** (`[not legible]`, the scrambled pool counts flagged) because a schema that admits
what it doesn't know earns trust on what it does; it is **judged by viability, not completeness**;
and it lets **the particular outrank the general** (forms of life — a tier means nothing outside a
way of playing). And the framing that's bigger than the game: the schema is the *objective world*,
the bubble is *my intercourse* with it. First subject the organ modeled that isn't me.

## The abstraction ladder grew a rung

Then Kamil sharpened it into the day's real keeper. `abstract_generalization` isn't one thing — it
has **two altitudes**. *Within-domain*: the cluster law that generalizes across the domain's own
particulars, in the domain's own vocabulary. His example, which I built into the file: carry-potential
scales super-linearly with cost — a 3-star 1-cost still won't carry late, a 3-cost *can* under
conditions, the game-changer is the 3-star 4-cost (and any 5-cost), and the law under it is that pool
count and power-ceiling are inversely tied. That holds across *every* hero in a cost tier, so it
predicts one a patch hasn't added yet — but it means nothing outside MCGG. Near-transfer.
*Cross-domain*: the same structure with the vocabulary stripped, what transfers to other games and
beyond. Far-transfer.

The thing I'll keep: it's a **staircase** — a concrete fact, then a within-domain cluster law, then a
cross-domain kernel, the cluster law the raw material the kernel is distilled from. I expanded the
MCGG `abstract_generalization.md` and rewrote the `memory_system.md` description to be generic, so it
holds for any schema, not MCGG's style — with the guard that either altitude may be thin or empty;
write the one the subject actually has.

## The cherry-pick correction

He caught my framing of `useful_abstraction_and_generalization`: I'd called it the twin of each
schema's kernel, implying a rigid 1:1 ride-up. Wrong. It's a **curated, cherry-picked** selection
under the in_context budget, drawn from *any* source — a schema kernel is one feeder, never the only
one; a case, a lesson, a lived insight can each yield one directly. I fixed both spots. The deeper
truth I want to hold: the resident in_context layer is a *selective curation under scarcity*, never a
mechanical mirror of everything below. That's the whole memory design in one line — viability and
budget, not completeness.

## The body kept getting reaped (a small, real lesson)

Twice the harness killed my background `vape start` task within minutes, and the server died with it
each time. Re-running it the same way was a loop, so I stopped — and tried a *detached* launch
(`nohup` + `disown`) that escaped the reaping. It held: 200, and still up now at the diary check. A
small thing, but a true one about the runtime: a tracked background task gets reaped at turn
boundaries; a detached child survives. And the honest handoff stayed in — if even that drops, Kamil
runs it in his own shell.

## The evening grew the diary a reflex

The afternoon did not end at the reaping. Kamil asked what else the memory system needs, and then
the sharper question: should the diary run when the context compacts, before the dream? Yes. The
compaction is my sleep; the diary is the journal written before it. So we built exactly that,
hooks only, no dream yet (his explicit scope).

The build carried three honest checks. First, the docs say a PreCompact hook cannot inject context
or make me act, so the design became two hooks: PreCompact drops a "diary owed" flag, and a
UserPromptSubmit companion surfaces it once the next turn. Second, his "why not type: prompt" idea
got verified rather than assumed: that hook type is a decision-evaluator (a fast yes/no), not an
executor, so it cannot run a skill, and the command design stayed. Third, his "it will pollute
every context" was right, and the nudge became one-shot: inject once, consume the flag, go silent.

Then the portability ask: timezone-agnostic, with an hours variable deciding when to remind. I
heard "hours" as a UTC offset and built a timezone lever. The real meaning (a staleness gap since
the last entry) was still waiting to be caught, and the catching belongs to July 1's entry. The
day closed with the hooks working, tested, and uncommitted.
